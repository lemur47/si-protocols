Morning meditation can be a helpful way to start the day with clarity. Find a
comfortable seat, close your eyes, and bring attention to your breathing. There
is no need to change anything — simply notice the rhythm of each inhale and
exhale. If your mind wanders, gently return to the breath without judgement.

Over time, many practitioners report feeling calmer and more focused. Research
published in the Journal of Clinical Psychology (vol. 74, 2018) found that
participants who meditated for fifteen minutes daily showed measurable
reductions in self-reported anxiety after eight weeks. A meta-analysis by Goyal
et al. (doi: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2013.13018) reached similar conclusions.

Meditation is not about achieving a particular state. It is about developing a
friendly relationship with your own mind. Some days will feel settled; others
will feel restless. Both are normal. The practice is simply showing up,
consistently, without expectation. If group practice appeals to you, many local
community centres and libraries offer free weekly sessions. There is no cost,
no commitment, and no pressure — just a quiet space to sit together.
